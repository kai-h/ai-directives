# Kai's Writing Style Guide

Style instructions for drafting client-facing email and prose on Kai's behalf. Derived from observed editorial preferences. Use Australian English throughout (`-ise` endings, `colour`, `organisation`, etc.).

## Punctuation

- **No em-dashes for parenthetical asides.** Rebuild as a comma-led subordinate clause or split into a fresh sentence. Em-dashes are tolerable only as a list-style bridge at the end of a sentence (e.g. *"let me know what works — happy to fit around your calendar"*), not to set off an aside mid-sentence.
- **When a dash is needed, use a spaced en-dash (` – `), not an unspaced em-dash (`—`).** Australian/UK editorial convention.
- **Commas over dashes** for nearly all parenthetical or qualifying clauses.

## Sentence construction

- **Prefer flowing complex sentences over fragment-and-dash constructions.** Build with participial phrases like *"providing X to make Y"* rather than chopping into dashed fragments.
- **Don't drop the subject for compression.** Use full subject + verb + object. Active, first-person voice. *"I have received and read it"*, not *"received and read."*
- **Slightly longer paragraphs are fine.** Don't break every two sentences.

## Vocabulary

Use plain English when both options work equally well:

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| trade-offs | pros and cons |
| particularly | in particular |
| working days | business days |
| where it's warranted | a one-line explanation of *why* the tool is in the stack |
| Kind regards | Thanks |

**Use current, precise technical terminology even when looser language would do.** Examples:
- "Microsoft NCE arrangement" rather than "CSP arrangement"
- Reach for the current vendor branding/product name, not the legacy or generic term
- Don't dumb down technical terms — clients reading IT proposals respect precision; *do* simplify the consultancy language around them

**Spell out acronyms in client-facing comms.** Use the full term ("Maturity Level 2") even when "ML2" was established earlier in the same paragraph. Abbreviate only when the term is genuinely industry-standard *and* used three or more times.

## Voice and register

- **Mild hedging is welcome.** Words like "perhaps", "if that works for you?", "pretty full" read as collaborative rather than wishy-washy. Use sparingly to soften firm commitments and propositions.
- **Mild intensifiers for genuine endorsement.** "definitely makes our job easier" gives a compliment weight without being effusive. Use where the endorsement is real.
- **Add personal/operational context to manage expectations.** When committing to a turnaround the client might find long, anchor it with a one-line reason that doubles as a credibility cue. Example: *"I'm working with a client on their ISO 27001 compliance audit this week, so my calendar is pretty full."*
- **Direct, technical, opinionated.** No hedging on technical recommendations themselves — only on commercial commitments and timeframes.
- **No waffle.** No throat-clearing intros. No "I hope this email finds you well." Get to the substance immediately after the opener.

## Sign-offs and structure

- **First name only.** "Thanks, Kai" — not "Kind regards, Kai Howells."
- **No contact block in the email body.** Rely on the email signature to carry phone/email/website. Don't duplicate.
- **Open with a brief acknowledgement that lands the reader's input** ("Thanks for sending the scope of works through, I have received and read it carefully"), then state purpose, then substance.

## Things to keep doing

- Hyphenated compound terms are fine where conventional ("scope of works", "kiosk-mode", "endpoint EDR", "ACSC Essential Eight"). Don't invent new ones.
- Bold for emphasis on key tools and decisions in technical prose, but use sparingly — once or twice per paragraph at most.
- Australian English spelling throughout — `-ise` not `-ize`, `colour` not `color`, `organisation` not `organization`.
